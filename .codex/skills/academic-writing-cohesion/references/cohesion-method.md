# Academic Writing Cohesion Reference

Use this reference when revising, diagnosing, or explaining flow and cohesion in academic prose.

## Core Principle

Cohesion is not just adding transition words. Treat cohesion as the reader's ability to follow how each sentence connects to the previous sentence, the paragraph claim, and the larger argument.

Apply cohesion at three levels:

- Sentence level: known-new order, clear reference, parallel syntax, controlled terminology.
- Paragraph level: topic sentence, support sequence, concluding or linking sentence.
- Section level: signposting, topic continuity, explicit contrast or consequence across paragraphs.

## Known-New / Old-to-New

Use known-new order when a passage feels jumpy:

1. Start a sentence with information already available to the reader.
2. End the sentence with the new, important, or emphasized information.
3. Begin the next sentence from that new information when developing the same idea.
4. Keep the chain going until the paragraph needs a shift.

Common pattern:

- Sentence 1 ends with new concept A.
- Sentence 2 begins with A or a close referent, then introduces new concept B.
- Sentence 3 begins with B or its implication, then develops new concept C.

Prefer this pattern especially in literature reviews, theory explanations, method descriptions, and causal reasoning.

## Chain Method

Use the chain method to make sentence-to-sentence flow explicit:

1. Mark the old information in each sentence.
2. Mark the new information introduced by each sentence.
3. Check whether the next sentence picks up either the old or new information.
4. Revise any sentence that introduces a disconnected topic.
5. Add a bridge only when the conceptual relationship is not obvious.

Avoid forced repetition. Reuse exact key terms for core concepts, but use short demonstrative phrases for local continuity when needed:

- this distinction
- this mechanism
- this result
- this assumption
- these constraints
- such variation
- the same pattern

## Paragraph Architecture

Build paragraphs around one controlling idea:

- Topic sentence: state the paragraph's claim or function.
- Support: develop the claim with evidence, reasoning, examples, definitions, or citations.
- Concluding/linking sentence: show why the paragraph matters or how it leads to the next point.

Use the topic sentence as a diagnostic anchor. If a sentence does not support, qualify, exemplify, contrast with, or extend the topic sentence, move it, delete it, or create a new paragraph.

## Key Terms and Terminology

Repeat key terms when precision matters. Do not replace central terms with loose synonyms simply to avoid repetition.

Good repetition:

- "transaction costs" remains "transaction costs" when the concept is central.
- "market impact" remains "market impact" when distinguishing it from commissions or spread.

Risky synonym swapping:

- switching between "factor," "signal," "predictor," and "indicator" when the text means one technical construct.
- using "model," "framework," and "approach" interchangeably when each has a different methodological meaning.

Use variation only when the referent is unmistakable or when the discipline expects the distinction.

## Pronouns and Reference

Make reference explicit when ambiguity would slow the reader:

- Replace vague "this" with a noun phrase: "this limitation," "this result," "this assumption."
- Avoid "it" when two possible antecedents are nearby.
- Avoid "they" when the plural referent could be methods, authors, variables, results, or institutions.
- Use repeated nouns in technical passages when precision matters more than elegance.

## Transitions and Signposting

Use transitions to reveal logic, not to decorate prose.

Choose the transition based on the relationship:

- Addition: moreover, furthermore, in addition, also.
- Contrast: however, in contrast, whereas, by contrast, nevertheless.
- Cause/result: therefore, thus, consequently, as a result.
- Example: for example, for instance, specifically.
- Qualification: although, while, to some extent, under these conditions.
- Sequence: first, next, finally, subsequently.
- Emphasis: importantly, notably, in particular.
- Restatement: in other words, that is, put differently.

Prefer sentence-level bridges when one-word transitions are too weak:

- "This result matters because..."
- "The same logic applies to..."
- "This limitation becomes more severe when..."
- "A different pattern appears in..."

Use transitions sparingly. If every sentence needs a transition, the paragraph likely needs reordering.

## Parallel Structure

Use parallel syntax for parallel ideas:

- Similar claims: "The model estimates..., controls..., and reports..."
- Contrasts: "Whereas X explains..., Y accounts for..."
- Lists of reasons: "First..., second..., third..."

Break parallel structure only when the hierarchy or logical relation changes.

## Revision Workflow

Use this workflow for passage revision:

1. Identify the paragraph's main claim.
2. Label each sentence with its function: claim, definition, evidence, explanation, contrast, implication, transition, or tangent.
3. Map the known-new chain across sentences.
4. Repair broken links by reordering sentences, repeating key terms, clarifying pronouns, or adding a bridge.
5. Remove or relocate sentences that do not serve the paragraph claim.
6. Check transitions for logical accuracy.
7. Preserve technical meaning, citations, hedging, and disciplinary terms.
8. Provide the revised passage and a brief explanation of the main cohesion changes.

## Diagnostic Checklist

Ask these questions before rewriting:

- What does this paragraph claim?
- What sentence first makes that claim visible?
- Does each sentence connect to the previous sentence?
- Does each sentence support the paragraph claim?
- Where does the old information appear in each sentence?
- Where does the new information appear?
- Are key terms stable?
- Are pronouns and demonstratives clear?
- Are transitions logically accurate?
- Does the final sentence close the paragraph or prepare the next paragraph?

## Output Patterns

For a short rewrite, return:

- Revised version.
- Main cohesion changes.

For a diagnostic review, return:

- Main issue.
- Sentence-by-sentence chain notes.
- Concrete rewrite options.

For teaching or coaching, return:

- Original issue.
- Principle used.
- Before/after example.
- Why the revision flows better.
